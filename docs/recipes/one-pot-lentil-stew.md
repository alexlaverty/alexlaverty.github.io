# One-pot lentil and cruciferous stew

A cheap, fast stew built around four health markers at once: blood sugar,
blood pressure, blood cholesterol, and long-term cancer risk. Everything
goes in a single pot, cooks in about 20 minutes, and leans on pantry
staples plus a handful of fresh vegetables. Red lentils are the base
because they cook in roughly 15 minutes with no soaking and carry a large
soluble-fibre and plant-protein load per serving. This is a companion to
[the most nutrient-dense single meal](most-nutrient-dense-meal.md), traded
down for speed and one pot.

## Ingredients

- **1 cup red lentils** — the base. Rinse first; no soaking needed.
- **3–4 cups water or low-sodium vegetable or bone broth** — keep the
  sodium down so the potassium in the dish is not cancelled out.
- **1 can diced tomatoes, undrained** — lycopene, cooked, with the juice.
- **1 onion, chopped, and 3–4 cloves garlic, crushed** — allium compounds.
- **1–2 cups broccoli or cauliflower, chopped** — cruciferous vegetables.
- **1 tsp turmeric, ½ tsp black pepper, 1 tsp cumin, 1 tsp oregano**.
- **2 large handfuls spinach or kale**, stirred in at the end.
- **Extra virgin olive oil and fresh lemon**, added to the bowl, not the
  pot.

## Method

1. Crush the garlic and chop the onion first, and let the crushed garlic
   sit for about 10 minutes before it goes near heat. Resting crushed
   garlic lets the enzyme alliinase form allicin, its main organosulfur
   compound, before cooking can deactivate the enzyme.
2. Rinse the lentils.
3. Put the lentils, water or broth, tomatoes, onion, garlic, broccoli or
   cauliflower, and all the spices in the pot.
4. Bring to a boil, then cover and simmer 15–20 minutes, until the lentils
   collapse into a thick porridge and the vegetables are tender.
5. Turn off the heat and stir through the spinach or kale; residual heat
   wilts it in about a minute.
6. Serve, then top each bowl with a drizzle of olive oil and a squeeze of
   lemon.

## Why these ingredients

**Blood sugar.** Red lentils are low on the glycaemic scale: their protein
and soluble fibre slow the release of glucose into the blood rather than
producing the spike a refined-carbohydrate meal would. The
[Mayo Clinic DASH guidance](https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/nutrition-and-healthy-eating/in-depth/dash-diet/art-20048456)
and general legume research both point to pulses as a steadying base for
blood-sugar control.

**Cholesterol.** The same soluble fibre binds bile acids and dietary
cholesterol in the gut and carries some of it out in the stool, so the
liver pulls cholesterol from the blood to make more bile. That is the
established mechanism behind soluble fibre's modest lowering of LDL
cholesterol.

**Blood pressure.** Lentils, tomatoes, and leafy greens are high in
potassium, which counters sodium and helps relax blood-vessel walls — the
core of the DASH pattern, which pairs high potassium and magnesium with low
sodium ([Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/nutrition-and-healthy-eating/in-depth/dash-diet/art-20048456)).
Leafy greens also supply dietary nitrates that the body converts to nitric
oxide, which widens blood vessels. Keeping the broth low-sodium matters
here; a salty broth works against the potassium.

**Cancer risk.** Three ingredients earn their place on the epidemiology:

- **Cooked tomato.** Tomatoes carry lycopene, which absorbs better cooked
  and with fat. Meta-analyses link high cooked-tomato intake to roughly a
  25–30% lower risk of prostate cancer, though the effect is modest and
  seen mainly at the high-intake end
  ([J Nutr Sci Vitaminol meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23883692/)).
- **Broccoli and cauliflower.** Chopping cruciferous vegetables releases
  glucosinolates, which form sulforaphane. Reviews link sulforaphane to
  lower risk across several cancers including prostate
  ([Cancers, 2021](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8508555/)).
- **Turmeric with black pepper.** Curcumin is poorly absorbed on its own. A
  1998 human study found that piperine from black pepper raised curcumin
  bioavailability by about 2,000% at 45 minutes
  ([Shoba et al.](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9619120/)). That headline
  figure has not been independently replicated and the clinical benefit is
  unsettled, but pairing the two costs nothing, so the pepper stays in.

## The activators

Two additions at the bowl do real work and are worth not skipping:

- **Lemon.** The iron in lentils and spinach is non-heme iron, which
  absorbs poorly on its own. Vitamin C from lemon juice in the same meal
  markedly increases how much of it the body takes up
  ([ACS Omega](https://pubs.acs.org/doi/10.1021/acsomega.2c01833)).
- **Olive oil.** The fat-soluble vitamins and carotenoids in the broccoli,
  tomatoes, and greens — including lycopene and vitamins A, E, and K — need
  dietary fat to absorb. A drizzle of extra virgin olive oil at the end
  supplies it, along with monounsaturated fat and polyphenols.

This is a nutrition write-up, not medical advice. Anyone managing blood
pressure, blood sugar, cholesterol, or medication interactions should check
with a clinician before making a dish like this a daily habit.
